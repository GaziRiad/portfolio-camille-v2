Payroll for ANNA Money app

I led experience design for a tool that generated a new stream of active users for ANNA Money.

My contribution:

Year

• UX Design
• Research
• UI Design (some of it)

2023

The challenge

The goal of this project was to rapidly release a solution for business owners to have their payroll filed accurately, cheaply, and without pain.

The highlights

The goal was to create a new stream of free account holders who could (in future) migrate to a paid ANNA plan. We discovered that business owners need a solution to have their payroll filed accurately, cheaply, and without pain, so we set out to deliver this and generate new account holders.

Project metrics

There were two key metrics we would chase on this project:

  1. Number of users creating new accounts for payroll.
  2. Number of these users who successfully filed a payroll with ANNA.

Upselling these users to a paid plan would be dealt with in a separate project.

Who was on board?

  • 2 x Product owners
  • 2 x Engineers
  • 1 x UX Design Lead – Camille
  • 1 x UI Designer (sometimes)

My contribution

I led UX solely and I created some UI as well – a UI designer was onboard initially to artwork important wireframes. The Head of Product Design signed off the final experience.

Time frame

We spent a total of 3 months on the design phase of this project. To fully engineer the solution and have it released took another 2 months.

Step 1

Discovery research

I synthesised customer research into a persona and I conducted additional research to more deeply understand our audience’s pain points around payroll.

Persona generation

I created a new persona from research I had already done as a larger project.

I focussed on Limited Company owners who might need to pay themselves a salary and pulled together their perspective on running a business.

This helped provide an understanding of who the user was, and their motivations, and I referred back to it during the design phase.

Additional research interviews

To deep-dive on pain points, I contacted customers in our customer base and asked them a few questions regarding their perspective on payroll.

Some specific problems came out of the conversations.

Step 2

Summarising the insights

From my two research initiatives I gained an understanding of our user and their pain-points.

Who is our user?

Profile — Micro business owner, 1 employee max, UK, a young business
Frustrations — Tasks taking a long time. Times can be tough. Expensive to hire an accountant. Don't want to overspend on taxes.
Motivations — Freeing up time to spend with the family, upskilling to be more self-sufficient as a business owner. Saving on costs.

What pain-points do users have regarding payroll?

I have no idea how to do it — I lack acumen on how to get set up and run payroll.
I don’t want to miss a deadline — I’m afraid of forgetting to pay the employee and HMRC on time.
I don’t know the right tax amount — I need confidence that I can pay the employee and the right tax amount to HMRC.

Step 3

The problem

Our product owner created a problem statement I was happy to work with, based on our research. In other projects, I’ve created problem statements, because they sharpen focus and help for ideation.

What pain-points do users have regarding payroll?

I need confidence that I can pay the employee and the right tax amount to HMRC.
Knowing I’m compliant and not missing a deadline
Saving money were possible, in terms of taxes and accountant cost
…So I can have peace of mind and focus on my core business!

Step 4

Competitive Analysis

Now that we knew the problem we would try to solve, the product owner and I both fed in with a number of competitive analysis tasks.

Competitive matrix

The Product Owner mapped out competitors and their feature set in a matrix.

Competitor deep dive

I researched products by trialing their platform or taking part in demos. Luckily, I needed a live tool for my own business, so it was great to connect with market offerings.

Step 5

Scrappy ideation workshop

As a very small cross-functional product team, I encouraged us to get together and conduct a Crazy-8’s exercise for our MVP goal.

Crazy 8’s

I led the small team in a Crazy 8 exercise, conducting idea generation on what a minimum viable product might look like, having users onboard, use a simple experience and subscribe to pay.

Affinity mapping

I took our ideas and organised them with the group using sticky notes (top right). This helped us identify our key ideas.

Sketching a high level flow

Taking our key ideas, we established as a group a high level user flow and the experiences that users would interact with (bottom right). This formed a hypothesis for our MVP.

Step 6

Outlining experiences

From having an awareness of the user, their broad perspective and their detailed problems that needed solving, I began designing.

User Journeys

I outlined the steps users would take to complete payroll tasks. The product owner and engineering lead approved these before moving to the next step. They served as a great understanding for everyone to grasp and collaborate on what we would be building.
